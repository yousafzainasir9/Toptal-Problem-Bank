2.1

What They Check

Toptal's Round 1 screener evaluates you on five criteria. Nail all five, and you advance. Fail one, and you're out.

1. English Fluency (B2+ Minimum, C1 Preferred)

They're listening for:

  • Comprehension: Can you understand accented English, complex questions, and idioms? Or do you keep asking them to repeat?
  • Fluency: Can you speak smoothly without long pauses, false starts, or grammatical errors that cloud meaning?
  • Pronunciation: Are you understandable? Heavy accents are fine if words are clear. Mumbling is not.
  • Vocabulary: Can you discuss technical concepts, experience, and rate negotiations confidently?
⚠ Reality Check: If you're B1 (lower-intermediate), you'll struggle to pass Round 1. Toptal clients are global but expect professional English. Spend 2-4 weeks improving before applying if you're weak here.

2. Motivation

They want to know: Why Toptal? Why now? Are you serious?

  • Don't say "I need money" or "I'm desperate." It kills your credibility.
  • Say: "I want to work with top-tier clients on challenging problems. Toptal's vetting means quality projects and premium rates."
  • Show you've researched Toptal. Mention the network, the matching process, or the quality of clients.
✓ Motivation Script: "I've been a C# developer for [X years]. I want to work on more complex, high-impact projects with skilled teams. Toptal's reputation for vetting and premium clients aligns with my goal to grow as a developer and earn fairly for my skills."

3. Relevant Experience

They ask: How many years of C#/.NET? Have you shipped production code? What projects?

  • Minimum: 3–5 years of professional C#/.NET development (varies by role).
  • Production Projects: Have you deployed code to live systems? Handled bug fixes, monitoring, deployments?
  • Scope: Were you a junior working on features, mid-level owning modules, or senior architecting systems?
✓ Experience Script: "I've worked 5 years in C#/.NET, primarily in [industry: fintech, e-commerce, etc.]. My last role involved building [feature/system], which I deployed and maintained in production. I've mentored 2 junior developers and led a small team of 3 on [project]."

4. Hourly Rate

They ask: What's your hourly rate? What are you expecting?

  • Too Low: $30/hr raises red flags. "Why so cheap? Are you inexperienced?"
  • Market Rate: C# developers on Toptal: $70–120/hr (depending on experience and market).
  • Too High: $200/hr without 10+ years and strong portfolio = unlikely to match with most clients.
⚠ Rate Strategy: Research your market (see Section 2.7). Quote a number that's fair but defensible. If asked, say: "I'm open to negotiation based on project scope, but my target is $[X]/hr for quality clients and long-term engagements."

5. Availability

They ask: Are you full-time, part-time, or available for hourly projects? Can you commit to 30+ hours/week?

  • Full-Time: 40+ hours/week, dedicated. Best for long-term engagements.
  • Part-Time: 15–30 hours/week. Fine, but limits your project options.
  • Timezone: Can you overlap with client hours? If they're US and you're EU, that's a plus.
ℹ Be Honest: If you're working another job, say it. Toptal respects honesty. But if you commit to 40 hours and only deliver 20, they'll terminate and blacklist you.

Common Questions & Model Answers

These nine questions appear in nearly every Round 1 interview. Prepare answers that are honest, concise, and compelling. Practice saying them aloud.

Question 1: "Tell me about yourself" (2 min)
This is your opening. Don't ramble. Cover: name, years of exp, tech stack, best project, current goal.
1

Model Answer:

"Hi, I'm [Name]. I'm a C# developer with 5 years of professional experience, primarily in web backends and microservices. I've worked with .NET Framework and .NET Core, SQL Server, and cloud platforms like Azure. My strongest project was building a real-time data pipeline for an e-commerce company—handling 10K requests/sec, optimizing database queries, and mentoring junior developers. Now, I'm looking to work with top-tier clients on more complex problems. That's why Toptal appeals to me."

✓ Keys: Specific (years, tech, company type), achievement-focused (real-time, scale, mentoring), future-looking (why Toptal).
Question 2: "Why Toptal?" (1–2 min)
They want to know you're not just applying to 10 platforms. Why Toptal specifically?
2

Model Answer:

"I've looked at freelance platforms, and most have a race-to-the-bottom on pricing. Toptal's different. You vet for quality, which means clients are serious, pay fairly, and expect real expertise. That's what I want—meaningful work with professionals, not commodity work. Plus, your 3% network gives me confidence in the projects and people I'll work with."

✓ Keys: Shows research, values alignment (quality over cheap), confidence in the brand.
Question 3: "What's your hourly rate?" (30 sec – 1 min)
Direct question. Have a number ready. Don't waffle.
3

Model Answer:

"My target is $85/hour. That's based on my experience level, the market for C# developers, and the value I bring. I'm open to discussing if there are project-specific factors, but that's my baseline."

✓ Keys: Confident, specific, justified, flexible. Don't apologize for your rate.
Question 4: "Describe a challenging project" (2–3 min)
Use the STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Show problem-solving and impact.
4

Model Answer (STAR):

Situation: "At my last job, we had a legacy monolithic app in .NET Framework running slow. Peak traffic caused timeouts. The team was stuck."

Task: "I was tasked with identifying bottlenecks and designing a migration strategy. This was critical for our contracts."

Action: "I profiled the code, found N+1 queries in the data layer, and proposed splitting the monolith into microservices. I led the effort: refactored the database layer using Entity Framework best practices, extracted a payments service, and deployed to Azure Kubernetes. I also mentored two juniors through the process."

Result: "Response times dropped 70%. The company reduced infrastructure costs by 30% and gained the ability to scale independently. The team shipped faster."

✓ Keys: Real problem, concrete action, measurable results. Shows leadership and technical depth.
Question 5: "What's your availability?" (1 min)
Be honest. Don't overcommit. "30+ hours/week" is typical for Toptal engagements.
5

Model Answer:

"I'm available full-time, 40 hours/week. I'm in [timezone], so I can overlap with [client timezone] during [hours]. I'm committed to long-term engagements, ideally 3+ months."

✓ Keys: Specific hours, timezone clarity, commitment language.
Question 6: "What technologies do you work with?" (2 min)
Be specific. Version numbers matter. Don't list irrelevant tech.
6

Model Answer:

"I'm strongest in C#, .NET 6+, and ASP.NET Core. Database-wise, I'm expert in SQL Server and comfortable with PostgreSQL. I've worked with Entity Framework, Dapper for data access. Cloud: Azure (App Services, Cosmos DB, Service Bus). Message queues: RabbitMQ, Azure Service Bus. I also know Docker and Kubernetes basics for containerization and orchestration. For testing, I use NUnit and Moq. In the last year, I've explored gRPC for microservices communication."

✓ Keys: Stack depth (core + adjacent), version-aware, honest about weaknesses ("basics" vs "expert").
Question 7: "Tell me about a technical disagreement with a colleague" (2 min)
Show maturity, collaboration, and the ability to advocate without being rigid.
7

Model Answer:

"We were debating ORM choice: my teammate wanted Dapper for performance, I advocated for Entity Framework for productivity. Both have merits. I made a POC showing Entity Framework with proper indexing and caching could meet our performance targets. We reviewed it together, and he agreed. We went with EF and added monitoring to catch slow queries. The result: we shipped faster, and performance was fine. It wasn't about me being right; it was finding the best solution together."

✓ Keys: Shows respect for others' views, data-driven thinking, collaborative outcome.
Question 8: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" (1–2 min)
Avoid "CEO" if you're applying as an IC. Show growth and learning mindset.
8

Model Answer:

"I see myself as a senior/principal engineer working on high-impact systems. I want deep expertise in cloud architecture and distributed systems. I'd also like to mentor other developers. Whether that's at a single company or through freelance work like Toptal, I'm less concerned—I care about the problems and the people. Right now, Toptal fits that because I can pick challenging projects and grow faster."

✓ Keys: Growth-focused, learning-oriented, flexible, aligned with Toptal's model.
Question 9: "Do you have questions for us?" (2–3 min)
Always ask. Shows you're serious and curious. Ask 2–3 questions.
9

Good Questions:

  • "What does the matching process look like?" How are developers paired with clients? How fast is it?
  • "What types of projects do you typically have?" Startups vs enterprises? Greenfield vs legacy? Duration?
  • "Are there any skill gaps I should address to improve my match rate?" Shows eagerness.
  • "What's the feedback process if an engagement ends?" Learning opportunity or reflection point?
! Bad Questions: "How much can I make?" (mercenary), "Is it hard to pass?" (doubt), silence (disengaged).
2.3

Round 1 Tips

Technical knowledge matters in Round 2–4. In Round 1, professionalism, confidence, and communication matter more. Here's how to optimize.

Setup & Environment

Webcam
• Turn it ON. Always. No exceptions.
• Eye contact: look at the camera, not the screen.
• Frame: head and shoulders visible, centered.
• Light: face well-lit, no shadows or backlight.
Audio & Environment
• Headset with mic (not laptop speakers).
• Quiet room. Close doors. Shut windows.
• No background noise: pets, kids, TV, traffic.
• Test audio before the call. Ask a friend to listen.
Background
• Clean, neutral background (home office, library).
• No mess, no embarrassing items visible.
• Virtual background OK if real one is bad.
• Avoid busy patterns. Solid colors better.
Appearance
• Dress professionally (shirt/blouse, no pajamas).
• Grooming: hair combed, face clean.
• First impression lasts. Invest 5 min in appearance.

Body Language & Tone

SMILE
Smile (Genuinely)
It's contagious and signals openness. Even on video, people feel it. Start with a smile and maintain it when listening.
PACE
Speak Clearly & Slowly
You're nervous. You'll speed up. Consciously slow down. Pauses are OK. They show thoughtfulness.
LISTEN
Listen More, Talk Less
Aim for 50/50 conversation. They ask, you answer in 1–2 minutes. Then you ask. Don't monologue.

Preparation Checklist

1
Write Down Key Points: Not full scripts—bullet points for 9 questions. Practice saying them aloud 3 times each.
2
Know Your Resume Cold: Every bullet. Dates, projects, metrics. Don't stammer or contradict yourself.
3
Prepare Your Tech Stack: List tools, frameworks, versions. Be ready to discuss depth vs breadth.
4
Set Your Rate: Research and decide. Don't waffle during the call.
5
Prepare Your Timezone & Availability: Write it down. Be specific: "9 AM – 5 PM CET" not "flexible."
6
Prepare 2–3 Questions: Don't wing it. Write them down. Show you've thought about Toptal.
7
Test Tech: 30 min before call: camera, mic, internet speed, lighting. No surprises.
8
Arrive Early: Join 5 min early. Compose yourself. Deep breath. You're prepared.
✓ Mindset: You're interviewing them too. You want a good fit. This isn't desperation; it's mutual selection. Confidence comes from that.

Questions You Should Ask

Asking questions signals engagement and helps you assess fit. Here are the best ones, grouped by topic.

About the Matching Process

Question Why Ask What to Listen For
"How does Toptal match developers with clients?" Understand the process. How much control do you have? Algorithm-based, manager-assisted, your preference considered? Fast or slow? Clear criteria?
"What's the typical timeline from approval to first project?" Expect income gap. How long before you earn? Days? Weeks? Depends on availability? Can you survive this?
"How often are developers matched with new projects if they finish one?" Job security. Is there downtime? Can you double-book? Reliable re-matching or gaps? Do 70% of devs stay engaged?
"What's the typical engagement length?" Plan your income. Short projects = unstable. Average 3 months? 6 months? Do they extend?

About Project Types

Question Why Ask What to Listen For
"What kinds of companies use Toptal? Startups, enterprises, both?" Understand client profile. Different work styles, budgets, and problems. Mix? Preference? Can you choose?
"Are there projects in [specific domain], like fintech or e-commerce?" Check if your interests align with available work. Range of projects available? Dead zones?
"What's the project size? Individual IC roles or team leads?" Understand scope. Do they value team contributions or solo work? Mix? Can you prefer one?

About Success & Growth

Question Why Ask What to Listen For
"What do top Toptal developers do differently?" Learn success patterns. Communication? Responsiveness? Expertise? Specific behaviors. Are they achievable?
"Are there opportunities to grow on Toptal? Mentoring, leadership roles?" Long-term vision. Is it just hourly work or career growth? Yes or no. Pathways or dead end?
"What's the feedback mechanism if an engagement ends?" Learn & improve. Or just move on? Structured feedback? Improvement plans? Blacklist?
✓ Golden Rule of Asking: Ask 2–3 questions max. Don't dominate. Each question should be thoughtful, not "what's the best rate?" or "how much money can I make?" Focus on fit and growth.

Common Mistakes

26.4% pass. Here's what the other 73.6% do wrong.

❌ Mistake 1: Talking Too Long
Answer a 2-min question with a 5-min monologue
Rambling without a clear point
Not leaving space for follow-up questions
Interviewer bored, disengaged by minute 15
✓ Right: Concise Answers
1–2 minute answers to most questions
Clear point, then stop
Pause and ask: "Does that answer your question?"
Interviewer appreciates clarity and respect for time
❌ Mistake 2: Being Negative About Previous Employers
"My last boss was terrible"
"The company was a mess"
"They didn't appreciate my work"
Red flag: gossip, blame-shifting, poor judgment
✓ Right: Constructive Perspective
"We had different visions for the tech stack"
"I learned a lot, but I'm ready for new challenges"
"The culture wasn't aligned with my goals"
Shows maturity, professionalism, and focus on future
❌ Mistake 3: Not Asking Questions
Silence when asked "Do you have questions?"
Or generic, unprepared questions
Signals low interest or lack of preparation
Missed opportunity to assess fit
✓ Right: Thoughtful Questions
"What does the matching process look like?"
"Are there projects in fintech available?"
"What helps developers succeed on Toptal?"
Shows engagement, thoughtfulness, genuine interest
❌ Mistake 4: Poor Audio or Technical Issues
Choppy connection, echoing
Can't hear the interviewer
Background noise (dog barking, phone ringing)
Unprofessional, disrespectful
✓ Right: Professional Setup
Test audio 30 min before. Use headset.
Quiet room. Close doors.
Good internet (hardwired if possible).
Professional, reliable first impression
❌ Mistake 5: Contradicting Your Resume
"I have 5 years experience" (resume says 3)
Vague about project details
Can't explain your tech stack
Caught in a lie—instant rejection
✓ Right: Consistent Story
Know your resume inside-out
Can discuss every project and role in detail
Dates, companies, achievements match
Credible, trustworthy candidate
! No Second Chances: One major mistake (lying, poor audio, being rude) and you're rejected. Toptal is filtering for professionalism. Be your best self.

English Fluency Guide

If English isn't your first language, here are tactics to sound more confident and fluent on the call.

Common Phrases for Technical Conversation

Situation Phrase Example
Buying time to think "That's a great question. Let me think for a second..." "...I'd say my strongest area is backend architecture."
Clarifying the question "Are you asking about [specific thing]?" "Are you asking about my experience with microservices?"
Admitting you don't know "I haven't worked much with [tech], but I learn quickly." "I haven't worked much with Go, but I've picked up languages fast in the past."
Transitioning topics "That reminds me of..." or "A related point is..." "That reminds me of a project where I faced a similar challenge."
Emphasizing a point "The key thing is..." or "What matters most is..." "The key thing is matching the right developer with the right client."

Filler Words to Avoid

❌ Weak Fillers (Use These Sparingly)
"Um" / "Uh" (nervousness)
"Like" (casual, unprofessional)
"You know" (repetitive, vague)
"Sort of" / "Kind of" (hedging, weak)
Excessive "actually" (sounds defensive)
✓ Strong Alternatives
Pause (silence > filler)
Clear transitions ("So", "Therefore", "In fact")
Confident language ("I believe", "I know")
Specificity ("This is important because...")
Direct statements (no hedging)

Pacing & Clarity

Slow Down
You're nervous. You'll speed up 30%. Consciously slow down 20%. This makes you sound confident, gives you time to think, and helps non-native accent listeners follow.

Trick: Place a sticky note: "SLOW DOWN" on your monitor.
Pause Between Sentences
Pause 2–3 seconds between ideas. It feels long to you, but to listeners, it's normal. Silence shows thoughtfulness.

Trick: Count "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi" between ideas.
Pronunciation Tips
• Emphasize first syllable: "PAR-a-digm" not "par-A-digm"
• Don't swallow endings: "technology" not "technolo-jee"
• Practice problem words before the call
• It's OK to have an accent. Clarity matters more.
Listening Actively
• Don't interrupt. Let them finish.
• Take brief notes. Show you're engaged.
• If you don't understand, ask: "Could you rephrase that?"
• Mirror back: "So you're asking about..."
✓ Reality: Toptal interviewers talk to people from 50+ countries. Heavy accents are fine. Clarity and confidence matter. Don't apologize for your English. Own it.

Rate Negotiation Guide

Know your worth. Research before Round 1. The rate you quote influences matching and income.

Market Rates by Experience & Region (USD)

Experience Level C# / .NET Factors
Junior (2–3 years) $40–$60/hr Limited experience, small portfolio, learning mindset
Mid-Level (4–7 years) $60–$95/hr Solid projects, mentoring experience, owns systems
Senior (8+ years) $95–$150/hr Architecture expertise, team leadership, rare skills
Principal / Specialist (10+ years) $150–$250+/hr Deep expertise, thought leadership, strategic advising

Regional Adjustments

Rates vary by geography, even for remote work:

  • US / Western Europe / Australia: Highest rates. +15–30% multiplier.
  • Eastern Europe / South America / Southeast Asia: Lower rates. -20–40% multiplier.
  • Canada: Similar to US (CAD slightly lower than USD equivalent).
  • India: Typically lowest. $20–$50/hr even for experienced devs (market-driven).
⚠ Reality: Toptal's matching takes geography into account. If you're in Asia but ask for US rates, you'll match with fewer clients. But don't undersell. Price-shop before deciding.

How to Calculate Your Rate

1
Check Market Data: Research on Toptal, Upwork, Stack Overflow, Payscale, Glassdoor. What are C# devs earning in your region?
2
Consider Your Experience: Years, projects, mentoring, tech depth. Be honest.
3
Add Freelance Premium (+20–30%): No benefits, taxes, overhead. Toptal rates should be higher than W2 salary hourly equivalent.
4
Example Calculation: Base salary in region: $60K/year → $30/hr (gross). Freelance premium: $30 × 1.5 = $45–$55/hr to start on Toptal. Adjust up as you gain experience.
5
Quote Your Rate: Round to nearest $5. $85/hr sounds better than $84.50/hr.
6
Expect Negotiation: Toptal may ask to adjust for project fit. Be open to $5–$10 discount for ideal projects. Don't undersell by 30%+.

Negotiation Tactics

If Asked to Lower Rate
Response: "I understand budget constraints. I can discuss $X/hr for a long-term engagement (3+ months). For shorter projects or part-time, I'd prefer my standard rate."

Shows flexibility without giving away value.
If Client Offers Higher
Response: "That's generous. I'm interested in the project quality and fit as much as rate. Let's talk about the project scope and expectations first."

Signals you're not mercenary; you care about good work.
ℹ Don't Rush Rate Negotiation: You can adjust your rate after the first engagement based on demand. Start fair, deliver great work, and raise your rate once you have feedback and history on Toptal.

Red Flags in Rate Discussions

  • Too Low Assumption: If your rate seems low, they might assume you're less skilled. Don't price yourself out of the market, but also don't invite doubt.
  • Vague Negotiation: If Toptal says "your rate is too high," ask: "What range are you targeting for this project?" Get specifics.
  • Scope Creep: If they ask to lower rate AND add responsibilities, push back. Scope and rate are linked.
✓ Final Mindset: You're a professional with proven experience. Your rate reflects that. Be confident, flexible, and results-driven. That's what sells.
→ Next Steps: Once you pass Round 1, you move to Round 2: Codility Test. Use the study plan from Part I to prepare. Round 1 gates access; Round 2 gates skill. Both matter.
